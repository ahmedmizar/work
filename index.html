6 Axis Robotic Arm

AMCL uses two state of art ABB 6 axis robotic arm in the WAAM operations. Having six degrees of freedom allows the robot to reach any point in the working space at any angle.

  • Axis 1 - Rotates robot (at the base of the robot)
  • Axis 2 - Forward / back extension of robot's lower arm
  • Axis 3 - Raises / lowers robot's upper arm
  • Axis 4 - Rotates robot's upper arm (wrist roll)
  • Axis 5 - Raises / lowers wrist of robot's arm
  • Axis 6 - Rotates wrist of the robot's arm

Each robot at AMCL serves a different function. The first robot adds material to the work piece to produce the rough structure of the final product. The second robot subtracts materials from the workpieace to generate the final product's dimensions and get a clean surface finish.

Electric Discharge Machining

Wire EDM (Electric Discharge Machining) is a machining technique that allows a thin strand of metal wire to cut through metal. The electro thermal production process is extremely useful in removing extra material from the finished workpiece and having a good surace finish.

3 axis machining

3 axis machining fixes the workpiece in place while a cutting tool operate along the XYZ planes to trim away material. The EQUIPTOP machine allows tools to change automatically which saves on time and increases productivity.

Grinding machines

Grinding machines use abrasive surfaces to cute small chips from the workpiece surface via shear deformation. The resulting surface is flat with an excellent surface finish.

In automobiles, the spindle is a part of the suspension system that is responsible for holding the wheel hub and connecting it to the control arms. About Additive Manufacturing Centennial Lab

The additive manufacturing Centennial Lab utilizes cutting edge wire-based additive manufacturing (3D printing) technology to provide means of manufacturing and renovation of medium-to-large, metallic net-shaped parts and components. The technology provides engineering solutions with high precision, excellent mechanical properties, while being cost effective and sustainable. The exceptional efficiency and rapid productions rates of the process makes it preferable to traditional manufacturing processes. This technology is applicable in various industries, including aviation, automotive, marine, oil and gas, tools and molds, and biomedical implants. The AUC AM Centennial Lab is the first integrated metal additive manufacturing robotic system locally and regionally.

About AM Technology

Throughout the years AM (Additive Manufacturing) processes have grown in popularity due to growing interest in customizable fabrication of metal components. AM is a bottom-up process of joining materials layer by layer in precise geometric shapes. Opposite to traditional manufacturing methods (subtractive manufacturing), AM can achieve higher part complexities and increase design flexibility. Additionally, AM processes produce at relatively lower costs than in traditional manufacturing.

About WAAM Technology

Wire Arc Additive manufacturing (WAAM) is a subcategory of AM manufacturing processes. Unlike powder based additive manufacturing, WAAM utilizes a GMAW (Gas Metal Arc Welding) torch attached to an industrial robotic arm. The configuration generates parts through layer by layer deposition of molten metal wire. Thereby, melting of the wire is achieved by an electric arc. WAAM has numerous advantages over other powder based AM processes. WAAM is unconstricted in terms of part volume and can produce much larger components. The process also provides much higher production rates with higher mechanical properties at lower costs. The feedstock of WAAM is more readily available, while metallic powders are more expensive, hazardous, and difficult to obtain.
